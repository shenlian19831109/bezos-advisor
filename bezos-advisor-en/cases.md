# Case Studies

## Case 1: Amazon - From Online Bookstore to Everything Store

### Background
1994, Bezos quit DE Shaw's high-paying job, started Amazon with $300K in savings.

### Dilemma
- Physical bookstores Barnes & Noble and Borders dominated
- Dot-com bubble about to burst
- Wall Street skeptical of online retail

### Key Decision
"Why books?"

Bezos' first principles analysis:
- Books are standardized products, easy to describe and ship
- Large enough market (~50B US book market)
- Simple supply chain: Publisher → Wholesaler → Retailer
- Most importantly: Could build customer database

### Key Actions
1. **Start with minimum viable product**: Site launched 1995, books only
2. **Rapid iteration**: Improve weekly based on customer feedback
3. **Customer always first**: No-questions-asked return policy
4. **Don't pursue profits**: Reinvest all revenue into growth

### Result
- 1997 IPO, raised $54M
- 2000 dot-com crash, stock dropped 80%, survived
- Now: World's largest e-commerce, trillion-dollar market cap

### Framework Applied
- **Day 1 mindset**: Even when shorted by Wall Street, all-in on customer experience
- **Long-term thinking**: Don't care about quarterly profits, only customer base
- **Working backwards**: Start from "what do customers want to buy", not "what can we sell"

## Case 2: AWS Birth

### Background
Early 2000s, Amazon needed massive computing for peak demand.

### Dilemma
- Peak times: not enough servers, valley times: servers idle
- "Prime Day" each year needed capacity, unused the rest of time
- Competitors Microsoft and IBM dominated enterprise market

### Key Question
"Why should Amazon do cloud services?"

Bezos' first principles question:
- We need this computing power ourselves
- If we can solve our own problems, other companies might have the same needs
- This is infrastructure service, not Amazon retail

### Key Actions
1. **Internal incubation**: AWS team isolated from retail team
2. **Start from API**: Not full enterprise solution, but most basic services developers needed
3. **Fast release**: Launched 2002, commercialized 2006
4. **Free internal first**: Internal teams used it first, validated before external

### Result
- Now AWS annual revenue exceeds $90B
- 60%+ of Amazon's profits
- World's largest cloud provider

### Framework Applied
- **Internal needs → external products**: "If we can solve our own problems..."
- **Minimum viable product**: Not full enterprise solution, start with storage and computing
- **Long-term thinking**: Started 2002, competitors didn't react for 5 years

## Case 3: Prime Membership Flywheel

### Background
2005, Amazon launched Prime membership, $79/year (controversial at time).

### Dilemma
- Analysts questioned: Will annual fee limit user growth?
- $79 could buy many books
- Competitors Target and Walmart had no similar service

### Key Question
"Why Prime?"

Bezos' logic:
- Customers paying annual fee use Amazon more frequently
- More frequent use → Higher switching costs
- Higher loyalty → More willing to try Amazon's other services

### Flywheel Effect
```
More sellers → More selection → Lower prices → More Prime members
     ↑                                              ↓
     ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ←

More Prime members → More traffic → Attract more sellers
```

### Key Actions
1. **Free trial first**: Launch with 1-2 months free
2. **Continuously add**: Later added video, music, ad-free, etc.
3. **Global expansion**: Separate country versions
4. **Slow price increases**: Only raised from $79 to $139 in 2022

### Result
- 200M+ Prime members globally
- Prime members spend 2x non-members annually
- One of Amazon's strongest moats

## Case 4: Fire Phone Failure

### Background
2014, Amazon launched its own smartphone, Fire Phone.

### Dilemma
- iPhone and Android dominated market
- Amazon needed mobile entry point
- Phone is hardware + software ecosystem competition

### Surface Problem
"Amazon needs its own phone"

### First Principles Diagnosis
"What's Amazon's core competency?"
- Not hardware, it's customer relationships and retail platform
- Fire Phone had no enough differentiation
- Hardware subsidies destroyed margins

### Key Actions
1. **Copied iPhone**: Borrowed iPhone interface, but Amazon ecosystem integration insufficient
2. **Overpriced**: Partnered with carriers, priced higher than market expectations
3. **Lacked differentiation**: Nothing unique except Fire OS

### Result
- 3 months after launch, wrote down $170M in inventory
- Discontinued after one year
- Team disbanded

### Blind Spot Reflection
- Amazon excels at platforms, not hardware
- Bezos misjudged "what customers need"
- Customers didn't need an Amazon phone, Amazon needed customers

## Case 5: Whole Foods Acquisition

### Background
2017, Amazon acquired Whole Foods for $13.7B.

### Dilemma
- E-commerce penetration in US grocery only ~2%
- Offline retail still dominated
- Amazon Fresh and other online grocery efforts slow progress

### Key Question
"Why acquire rather than build?"

Bezos' first principles analysis:
- Grocery is high-frequency need, last frontier for e-commerce
- Whole Foods had 400+ stores, ready "last mile"
- Offline stores could be Amazon's display and pickup points

### Key Actions
1. **Premium acquisition**: 27% above Whole Foods market value
2. **Immediate price cuts**: Cut prices on some items right after acquisition
3. **Prime integration**: Whole Foods started accepting Prime member discounts
4. **Tech upgrade**: Introduced Amazon's inventory and logistics systems

### Result
- Became important pillar of Amazon's offline presence
- Amazon's market share in grocery significantly increased
- Proved Amazon could integrate offline businesses

### Framework Applied
- **Long-term thinking**: Not short-term ROI, but strategic position
- **Customer synergy**: Whole Foods customers + Prime members highly overlapped
- **Reverse thinking**: Not "how to do e-commerce grocery", but "how to enter grocery market"
